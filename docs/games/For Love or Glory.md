# For Love or Glory

Below is a new, literal English translation of the complete text you supplied. I have omitted only the repeated board-coordinate diagrams. The translation favors meaning over rhyme and meter; several archaic or damaged readings remain necessarily approximate.

## Chess of Love

Francesc de Castellví, Narcís Vinyoles, and Bernat Fenollar

Valencia, c. 1474–1475

### Prologue

A work entitled Chess of Love, made by Don Francí de Castellví, Narcís Vinyoles, and Mossèn Fenollar under the names of three planets—namely Mars, Venus, and Mercury—through whose conjunction and influence it was invented.

Don Francí de Castellví conducts the game of Mars. His battle cry is Love, and he carries the red standard. His King is Reason; his Queen, Will; his Rooks, Desires; his Knights, Praises; his Bishops, Thoughts; and his Pawns, Services.

Narcís Vinyoles conducts the game of Venus. His battle cry is Glory, and he carries the green standard. His King is Honor; his Queen, Beauty; his Rooks, Modesty; his Knights, Disdains; his Bishops, Sweet Glances; and his Pawns, Courtesies.

Mossèn Fenollar declares the effects of Mercury. First, he divides the board and compares it to Time. He totals the number of squares, which are sixty-four, corresponding to sixty-four stanzas, and gives the laws and agreements that the players must observe.

All the stanzas are linked, with nine lines in each, following a sequence of four, three, and two lines; thus they should be written and read. In their concluding epitaph you will see their literal meaning gathered together: namely, the game of chess and its rules.

⸻

### I. The armies are assembled

**Castellví — Mars**

Finding Mars with Venus in a temple,
with Mercury also in their presence,
he devised a game of chess in a new fashion:
taking Reason as King, without superiority;
Will as Queen, endowed with great power;
Thoughts he appointed as his Bishops;
Knights were Praises, with sweet eloquence;
Rooks were Desires, which set memory aflame;
Pawns were Services, fighting for victory.

**Vinyoles — Venus**

To exercise her glory, Venus chose
cautious Modesty as her Rook;
Disdains as Knights, offering deserved repayment;
Sweet and delightful Glances as Bishops;
as Queen she chose gracious Beauty;
and her King, following love's history,
was Honor, whose life stood in danger;
as faithful Pawns she took Courtesies,
armed and adorned with a thousand appearances.

**Fenollar — Mercury**

Mercury, swift in his manners and courses,
painted the board in alternating light and darkness;
and Time, divided into nights and days,
formed a square within the first boundaries.
He divided it by a cross into four regions
of differing time; and afterward, following his guides,
made four equal figures within each quarter.
Dividing the remainder under the same agreement,
the completed count amounted to sixty-four.

⸻

## The Game

### 1. e4 d5

**The King's Pawn moves to the fourth square**

The field divided and all the people ready,
the great warrior beneath the red standard
at once set all his host in motion,
taking Love as the name of his cause.
Toward the lady's field he dispatched
the bravest Pawn of the conquest,
who advanced two steps toward her.
By moving him, he uncovered the King, Reason,
and opened the road for Will.

**The Queen's Pawn moves to the fourth square**

The gracious Lady, not poor in courage,
carrying the green banner of hope,
cried out as her name: "Glory! May glory
cover my people with blessed fortune!"
Her courteous Pawn, with great restraint,
advanced so that Beauty might open,
in the game of love, the first encounter.
Assuming a humble gesture for his defense,
he struck the heart with an assault of love.

**The touched piece must be played**

The first law that must be accepted
is that, in such a game, the piece touched
must certainly, firmly, and without
argument or dispute, be played.
This stands to reason, for a thought in love,
once it has made its choice, must not remain
free or suspended, but wholly subjected.
Therefore it is said: "Boldness and folly—
once the deed is done, let it remain done."

⸻

### 2. exd5 Dxd5

**The King's Pawn takes the Queen's Pawn**

The good Service, received with Courtesy,
gathered new courage to accept her,
making himself master of the place where he welcomed her,
without further thought of whether he might trouble her.
For the gratitude that compelled him to love her
moved him to desire such glory,
always following love until he found her.
Whoever undertakes a thing and would succeed
must not stop for any fold or wrinkle in the road.

**The Queen, on her fourth square, takes the Pawn that captured hers**

So that the fame of Beauty might shine,
seeing that Service had tasted Courtesies,
she came forth from her house with great severity
to punish such foolish fantasies.
Caring nothing for his many flatteries,
she looked around as the crane watches a field
and killed the Pawn who had shown such boldness.
Before undertaking anything, one must consider
lest the end of the deed rebuke its maker.

**A captured piece remains captured**

The next law, which we wish both players to understand,
is that a person captured remains a captive.
One must therefore think carefully with whom
Will becomes involved, since she is a lofty lady.
For whoever avoids the greatest blows
may afterward be brought lowest—let the player learn this—
because understanding is willingly made captive.
If Will refuses to please Reason,
it is fitting that we should see the captive fall.

⸻

### 3. Nc3 Qd8

**The Queen's Knight moves to the Bishop's third square, attacking the Queen**

Will, desiring to please
the chosen Lady as much as she could,
resolved to send toward her
her great Praise, with most perfect speech.
It was the counsel of a discerning person
always to think of serving, and never displeasing,
the one whom we have made mistress of the heart.
Foolish is he who dares commit an outrage
against one to whom he owes vassalage.

**The Queen returns to her place**

To guard well the tower of homage,
the gracious Lady returned to her place;
for a Knight, especially one of noble birth,
must not wish to stain an honorable reputation.
When passion sets the lover aflame,
he must not injure the possessions of honor;
and should he wish to do so, retreat breaks the plot.
For when it rains, one who shelters beneath a leaf,
thinking himself dry, is soaked twice over.

**A player who has indicated a square must move there**

The third decree requires everyone to settle
within his castle or conquered square,
and never afterward abandon it by choice;
rather, he must die there with sword in hand.
For Will is kindled by such a fire of love
that no force can ever strip away her prize;
she is a lady who destroys every faculty.
Since Will is never won without consent,
she cannot change until consent withdraws.

⸻

### 4. Bc4 Nf6

**The King's Bishop moves to the fourth square opposite the Queen's Bishop**

Reason, finding sharper than a blade
the Lady whom he had considered most humble,
remained deeply saddened by such deception,
seeing that she disdained Praises and Services.
Because her worth greatly invited him,
he sent his sighing Thought
as a spy as far as the fourth degree.
Whoever wishes to leap high in order to rise
must first lower himself a little.

**The King's Knight moves to the King's Bishop's third square**

The Honesty with which great Beauty
usually adorns herself, if she would be divine,
sent forth her Disdain without delay
to cut off the mine dug by Thought.
For should Thought decline from virtue
and refuse ever to limit itself,
the defense of Honor undermines it.
Fire always burns according to its nature,
but water makes it dance according to its measure.

**On a King's first move, he may leap to the third square**

The next decree, which greatly refines the game,
is that the Kings, on their first move,
may freely leap to the third square
in whatever manner, without further excess;
showing that only reluctantly should we leave
the level ground or the secure road for the hillside.
Such an example is expected from them in all things.
Therefore, when anyone exceeds his limits,
he errs—but only a little, if he does not linger there.

⸻

### 5. Nf3 Bg4

**The King's Knight moves to the Bishop's third square**

The lover, seeing that neither pride nor anger
passed from the gracious lady,
had to employ Reason, who guides the faithful;
and she willingly became his great supporter.
She sent him her Sweet Speech,
which charms the people, without long delay,
so that it might reach the one the world adores.
Oh, how valuable to us, although it costs so little,
is that sweet sound composed from Praise!

**The Queen's Bishop moves four squares to the opposing King's Knight**

A bitter invitation covered with a sweet crust
is Flattery, which soothes all the senses;
and, flattering Beauty, it approaches pleasantly
the false deception hidden beneath it.
Drawing from her eyes, as love's reward,
a Sweet Glance in place of an answer,
she heals the wound caused by her Disdains.
The eyes can behold the invisible
and torment the spirit that cannot suffer.

**When making the three-square leap, the King cannot capture or give check**

So that Kings, though possessed of invincible power,
may not stretch forth their hands beyond reason,
it is decreed that in making the permitted leap
they may neither give check nor capture.
This shows the world that in punishing or rebuking
they must mingle mercy with anger
and not do all that their power allows.
For if they are great through the wings of power,
they will be greater still by exercising clemency.

⸻

### 6. h3 Bxf3

**The King's Rook Pawn advances one square against the Bishop**

Seeing the look of such a beautiful countenance,
good Desire, who serves beneath Reason,
immediately sent forward, with great reverence,
his Service to receive the one who stirred him.
Affection, written within the eyes,
awakens the heart through the harmony
of the lover in whom true love dwells.
Conformity is the surest thing
for joining friendship between those who love.

**The Queen's Bishop takes the King's Knight**

Because Praise awakens the spirits
and softens Beauty's expression,
the Sweet Glance, which strikes the heart unerringly,
captured Praise, in which hope is enclosed.
This signifies that the lover never lacks
the promised end of love
so long as the fountain of praises never dries.
For flatteries make a lady so content
that even discontent appears contented.

**The King may not leap over other pieces**

It was further agreed that Kings, in whom
such great worth resides, may not leap over others,
but must always travel upon secure roads
where thieves can never assault them.
For since our honors are burnished by Kings,
if they leave behind those who can support them
and guard themselves poorly, they fail themselves and the people.
Nature requires the limbs to defend themselves
from injury to the head, in which all suffer harm.

⸻

### 7. Qxf3 e6

**The Queen takes the opposing Queen's Bishop**

With a radiance that repays great love,
the eyes, so lively that their gaze set things aflame,
invited Will most humbly
to the joyful song of the enkindled Praise.
They gave her such courage through their appearance
that she at once took, as payment and compensation,
the rays of the Sun that pierced her so deeply.
The eyes are stars by which sailors navigate
when they drown in this sea.

**The King advances his Pawn one square**

So that lovers might more clearly understand
with what restraint Honesty holds
the Thought by which they allow themselves to be blinded,
she courteously closes the doorway against them,
moving the Pawn only one step as a guard.
The enemies unfold their squadrons,
seeing their hope already dead before them.
Discretion courteously brings order
when desire brings foolish disorder.

**A King who retreats may not make the three-square leap**

The eighth law is that a valiant King,
when withdrawing his person,
may never make the said leap,
for he is born from Honor and must honor the high crown.
Rather, step by step, which better suits him,
he must return along his road without fear or pain,
with the gravity that crowns such princes.
For a discreet retreat is worth far more
than being captured through unrestrained haste.

⸻

### 8. Qxb7 Nbd7

**The Queen takes the Queen's Knight Pawn**

But appetite for the fruit of love
so moved unfettered Will
that she entered Venus's field to fight
a Courteous one who attempted to show disdain.
This was especially because he bore the stamp
of the one who had already cooked her in love's fire:
great Beauty, who tempts even the wisest.
When one sees that smoke has already become flame,
everyone should take care, for poison is spreading.

**The Queen's Knight moves two points from the Queen**

Beauty, seeing Will inflamed
against Disdain, capturing her Courtesy
and threatening modest Reputation,
moved gracefully to defend herself.
Leaping with her Knight, she offered battle
against the Lady's army,
which was attacking Modesty with force.
It is a sure example that disaster never follows
the one who knows how to discover its trail.

**The King is not captured or struck, but warned by check**

Another law follows along this path:
the King must not be treated like the others.
Should some obstacle be placed against him,
or should the danger of the enemy aim at him,
warn him, so that no crossbow may strike him.
Thus the heavenly planet decrees,
having clothed him in royal garments.
The name of King terrifies enemies:
he defends the good among his own and banishes the wicked.

⸻

### 9. Nb5 Rc8

**The Queen's Knight moves to the fourth square of the opposing Queen's Knight**

War now completely broken out between them,
the great Queen who proclaims the fair name of Love
ordered a Knight to come swiftly
into that part of the field,
so that with her people more closely united
he might conquer the enemy Courtesy
that stood poorly distributed across the field.
Whoever does not resist the first assault
will be unable to resist at the last.

**The Queen's Rook moves to its Bishop's square**

Modesty is the gold in which Beauty is set;
therefore she placed herself near Beauty in the watchtower,
abandoning—since she saw Flattery corrupting it—
the Courteous Manner, which was already faint with fear.
For Honesty, when it is not properly guarded,
suffers injury within the chaste person
when Flatteries are allowed inside the enclosure.
Fear suits the bird that is on the wing,
since it keeps it from falling into the net.

**After receiving check, the King must move or be covered**

Once the King has been warned of unsuspected danger,
another law requires him to be covered or to move.
A courageous man does not fear the enemy,
yet he must not allow contempt for him to cause injury.
Therefore, prudently, let him close the road to danger,
for every day we see the great lion
defeated by the weasel, even beneath a royal robe.
We are all equal in death and in birth;
therefore our fear should likewise be equal.

⸻

### 10. Nxa7 Nb6

**The Queen's Knight takes the Queen's Rook Pawn**

Seeing Modesty move to strengthen
Honor, Courtesy, and Beauty,
the Knight, with good composure,
entered the battle without further fear,
breaking the shield with which she adorned herself:
Fear of committing a dishonorable fault,
which is the passion of one who values the Lady.
The victor, while conquering, must watch how he strikes,
for the defeated often wins when he turns.

**The Queen's Knight moves to the third degree of its line**

When she can do no more, Beauty
defends her possessions with an Outraged Look.
If Praise moves or redirects her people,
she remains firm, constant, and unmoving.
Thus, if the whole resides in the nobler part,
and her Disdain should seem to turn out of the street,
according to common judgment,
the discreet man says, seeing an extreme action:
"He knows well what he does who burns his own house."

**Each player must take one turn and may not make two moves in succession**

Reason must be whole and not diminished,
and must be reasonable toward everyone in everything.
Therefore, in the game, each plays in turn:
one first, whatever seems praiseworthy to him;
the other afterward responds with a notable move.
Let each make his stroke, whether he attacks, waits, or fears,
and let them proceed equally in harmonious turns.
No one, while winning, should think to cross the line,
for order must be observed in battle.

⸻

### 11. Nxc8 Nxc8

**The Knight takes the Queen's Rook**

The Knight, breaking the tether entirely,
struck down Modesty and drew so near
to noble Beauty that, with joy and laughter,
he wished to die in the green meadow like the swan.
And if he dies contentedly, though by an unworthy death,
Will must not begin a quarrel,
since the Knight has become a gentle lamb.
Every day one sees death in this combat;
therefore love's suffering should not be lightly esteemed.

**The Queen's Knight takes the opposing Queen's Knight**

Having lost Modesty, which is the summit
of Honesty, fragrant as the lily
and a strong file that files away all vileness,
Outrage moved, burning more fiercely than cautery,
against Flattery, inflicting such torment
that suffering refined false bitterness
and made from its sweet juice a soothing medicine.
Beauty must defend herself and be sparing,
for she is valued more dearly the more dearly she costs us.

**No false exchange may be played, such as Knight for Rook**

It is also agreed, though not yet stated,
that one power must not seize another unjustly.
Therefore, let each weigh his rights correctly
and not stretch his foot beyond his power,
but play justly, lest the observer's eye
rebuke him; for a heavy burden is prepared
for anyone who would force his Knight upon a Rook.
The priest must not desire worldly pomp,
nor the knight anything that corrupts honor.

⸻

### 12. d4 Nd6

**The Queen's Pawn moves to the fourth square**

The formation broken, so that it might be broken further,
the victor continually encouraged his people
with cries and the sound of a spirited trumpet,
increasing their courage and strength.
Will, seeing Beauty turn away,
presented her—so the game would not be interrupted—
with a new Service to reinforce their friendship.
The game of love must be played with skill;
whoever tries to win it by force does not win.

**The Queen's Knight moves to the Queen's third square**

The Queen, seeing how poorly Will
accompanied the Service she had left,
sent forth a great Disdain with a strange expression
to outrage the ambush she perceived,
and especially because she thought and believed
that through it she could drive away the other companion
created by Thought or Will.
When the enemy pretends to abandon the field,
flee as well, for art must answer art.

**A Pawn may not pass another Pawn without battle**

A fair additional edict is proclaimed:
when Pawn plays against Pawn,
they must fight person against person,
and otherwise may not pass one another.
But if one remains with heavy lance at rest
while the other abandons him, let him advance
and gather courage to carry him to a better port.
Knights are restrained by honor,
but common men secure themselves through force.

⸻

### 13. Bb5+ Nxb5

**The King's Bishop moves four squares toward the opposing Queen's Knight and gives check**

Thoughts, which scold and murmur
when they see Will desiring and acting,
conspire, troubling Honesty,
so that they may not remain neglected and poor.
These are the masters and laborers
who refine every evil and opposition
and serve as lamps for the light of love.
Whoever hastens the hunt in agitation
either catches nothing, falls, or becomes entangled.

**The Queen's Knight takes the King's Bishop**

The Outraged Gesture, seeing Thought threaten
Honesty and strongly forcing her to rise,
resolved that it would rather die in the field
than allow an evil Thought to feed on Honor.
It rightly shows that whenever
some false accusation arises, one should not be moved by it,
since truth absolves one from guilt.
The proverb says: "Whoever ties up a healthy finger
can untie it safely, since nothing is wrong with it."

**King may not fight King**

Another ancient law must be observed:
King may never fight against King;
each must remain among his own people,
for the heads must never engage in combat.
It belongs to the others to strike and repel;
to them belongs command, so that the King may order
his own people to destroy false enemies.
It is a servile act, unworthy of royal footsteps,
for Kings to butt one another like rams.

⸻

### 14. Qxb5+ Nd7

**The Queen takes the Queen's Knight and gives check**

Will, seeing that cruel Disdain
had attacked her cruelly so many times,
condemned and banished him with great contempt
in order to end their encounters.
She then closely courted Honor,
asking her without lengthy embassies
to give herself to the one who thirsted so greatly for her.
Whoever wishes to conclude a matter must use everything:
speech, action, thunder, lightning, and rain.

**The King is covered by the Knight**

Royal authority must never be moved
except in a very great and necessary case.
Thus Honor resolved to enclose himself,
greatly disdaining the adversary's intention.
Such is the payment for this kind of service,
for the Courteous Manner might cause harm
in such an offense and become strongly opposed to him.
In a close game, courtesy, gravity,
and pomp cease under pressure.

**Smothered mate**

If, besieged by adverse fortune,
the King and all his power
are trapped with him, we expressly decree
that he must die sadly, since he can expect no relief.
For the royal crown and order
presuppose the highest liberty;
if he loses it, he loses all hope.
Dignity is joined to the soul,
and honor lives much longer than life.

⸻

### 15. d5 exd5

**The Queen's Pawn moves four points toward the opposing Queen**

So that Beauty might always be served,
good Will, disregarding Outrage,
ordered his Service, a gracious page,
to advance with infinite love.
Giving every advantage freely,
with a humble gesture he invited Courtesy
to take his life as the toll for passage.
Humility relieves, purges, and reconciles
two hearts stained in very different ways.

**The King's Pawn takes the opposing Queen's Pawn**

A Courteous man whose purse is thin
never rejects a gift;
or, if he has done so, his heart forever regrets it
because appetite is dry and desires rain.
Thus he takes it at once, lest it escape,
to build a castle from his little hut;
but when it fails him, he grows even more bitterly annoyed.
Whoever takes must think about repayment,
for when gratitude compels, recompense is also compelled.

**Bare or robbed mate**

If the King no longer has people to defend him,
having defended his vassals so poorly,
let him surrender when Fortune decrees
that he remain alone, all his people captured.
For Kings without people are not admitted as Kings.
Let good courage, strengthened by virtue, compensate
for the cruel fates that have overwhelmed them so strongly.
Fortune must always be shared by King and people,
in evil and in good, among them all.

⸻

### 16. Be3 Bd6

**The Queen's Bishop moves to the King's third square**

To gather all her force into one place,
Will set her Thought
before Reason, placing no value
on anything lost—Services or other things—
leaving the bare text and basing herself upon its meaning,
solely to serve that one and only lady
who had taken Honor for her husband.
If good Will paints love's image,
neither life nor death can ever erase it.

**The King's Bishop moves to the Queen's third square**

The Lady said, singing in a clear voice:
"Be watchful, Honor!" while the Eyes kept guard,
reading the red ink of love.
Awake and circling, since the hour seemed late,
they kept watch; for by guarding herself,
great Beauty, girded with such a belt,
might remain secure within the protected place.
This combat is won in such a manner
that the victor flees and the defeated waits.

**Ordinary checkmate**

Another decree confirms and declares
that if the King has nowhere to retreat,
nor anyone to cover him, and the enemy prospers
so greatly that he can strike him with a mortal wound,
then, when he dies, he gives his body as payment,
and his people, left without a banner,
fall into servitude, since life still pleases them.
For amid the dangers of bitter battle,
the eye provides more defense than the shield gives cover.

⸻

### 17. Rd1 Qf6

**The King's Rook moves to the Queen's square**

Seeing Beauty defend herself so strongly,
deprivation stirred appetite and caused
Desire to come forth unburdened
and take Will's place as his resting point.
Will drives him, but delight is his final cause;
and if the end is delayed,
his burning never ceases and never rests.
All movement begins from some deficiency
and continues until it reaches the desired object.

**The Queen moves to the King's Bishop's third square**

Desire beginning to produce its effect
came to the place where Will had been accustomed
to dwell freely, so that by a direct path
it might move wherever Beauty guided it.
She, with the light of grace, placed herself
in the third degree of Honor's
Delightful Aspect, which stood in danger.
Beauty must adorn herself with virtues
if she wishes to be adored by everyone in the world.

**The Queen may move like every piece except the Knight**

But our game wishes to adorn itself anew
with a novel style, strange to one who considers it,
taking the orb, the scepter, and the throne;
for above all, the Queen causes herself to be honored.
Therefore, since it is said that she is worth more and moves farther,
she may travel freely throughout the whole field,
but may not bend her course from fear or anger.
The more exalted freedom sees itself to be,
the more it should fear one day falling captive.

⸻

### 18. Rxd5 Qg6

**The King's Rook takes the Queen's Pawn**

Seeing that the Lady no longer withdrew,
good Desire fed upon Courtesy.
He became inflamed with such a living fire
that he wished to burn the greenest forest without pause;
for hope relieved him from suffering,
and it is a delight that gives strength new life,
making Will heir to an ugly end.
The fire of love requires very green wood:
when the wood is dry, the more it burns, the more it protests.

**The Queen moves to the King's Knight's third square**

Beauty, carrying Courage as her standard
to punish deceitful Services,
placed herself before the Disdain that disdains.
Honor loses greedy favors,
for when the battles of love gracefully
deliver their blows, who could be made of stone
and not quickly surrender to delightful acts?
A single "no" from a chaste person is worth more
than every "yes" that vice can grant.

**A Pawn may not be promoted to Queen, and there may be only one Queen**

If the ancients, to increase the lineage,
without regard for law or justice,
allowed a thousand Queens of low blood
and common clay to be created through malice,
the laws of love's jurisdiction declare
that the diamond is set in the finest gold
and shines most clearly in a single setting.
The faithful lover loves only one;
the faithless ingrate worships idols.

⸻

### 19. Bf4 Bxf4

**The Queen's Bishop moves four points toward the King's Bishop**

Will, who honors the Sweet Eyes
and comments upon their delicate gaze,
raised and enamored her Thought
toward those in whom living love sparkled.
Through the ardor it received and drew from them,
it distilled liquid from its own eyes,
which troubled the heart with reports
that wandered unsteadily within the head.
Love is one thing joined through the eyes,
but when the bond tightens, it threatens both part and whole.

**The King's Bishop takes the Queen's Bishop**

Thought, which pursued the Sweet Glance,
set itself against it in the manner of a quarrel
and was captured and killed, so that it might do no further harm
or cause blind Will to act.
For she drives herself on when longing spurs her,
entangling and obstructing the possessions of Honor,
and, washing the spirits, suffocating them.
Honor lives only so long as Will permits it;
she alone is the doorway to both good and evil.

**The Queens may not capture one another**

Since our new style transfers all power
from the loving Kings to the beloved Queens,
and just as Kings are not permitted to strike one another,
the Queens shall likewise be accorded this privilege.
They may, however, be attacked by the other pieces
and captured if they carry no escort;
only in this respect do they remain limited.
Therefore the law of our school rightly says
that the Lady must never be left alone.

⸻

### 20. Qxd7+ Kf8

**The Queen takes the Queen's Knight and gives check**

Good Worth was not at all discouraged
by Thought's destruction in such a crisis,
but, encouraged by Desire, chose and flew
against Disdain, the diminishment of Honesty.
Then, raising a voice clearer than crystal,
he at once made Disdain tremble in fear
on seeing Favor already written in his letter.
Perfection takes its form from the end;
therefore love is shaped by the good it desires.

**The King withdraws to his Bishop's square**

Honesty fashioned her great cry
with these words: "O exalted Venus!
Since your power does not restore mine
and your help fails me at this moment,
Worth strips the enamel from my power.
Seeing that yours no longer agrees with mine,
my Virtue escapes as far as it can by flight."
Though such terrors hardly suit Kings,
they are natural and sustain their being.

**If the Queen is lost, the game is lost**

Mercury declares that, since such Queens
understand so much and can do and command all things,
if by misfortune the enemies capture them,
the armies must fall and be laid low.
Therefore let them take care not to be deceived,
for if through fear they fail to defend themselves
with their strength, they can neither spin nor wind thread.
Modesty and fear are virtues in a lady;
in a man they are hindrances opposed to great fame.

⸻

### 21. Qd8 mate

**The Queen gives mate on the opposing Queen's square**

Prince Mars, who sets our hearts aflame,
in triumph over so lofty a conquest,
captured Honor, which cries out above all things,
and readily offered it to good Worth.
