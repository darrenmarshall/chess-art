A study of the game of chess, and the tension, momentum, skill, and nerve that emerges from plotting the notation record.

→
Exhibition statement

Every game of chess leaves a drawing behind — thirty or forty lines, a handful of red crosses, and nothing else. These ten survive.

How the works are made

Each game is scored as a triptych. White's moves are drawn on a white ground, seen from White's side of the board; Black's on a black ground, from Black's side; the full game on the winner's ground, from the winner's perspective. Every move is a single straight line from departure square to arrival square.

How to read them

A red × marks every square where a player took an opponent's piece — crosses only ever appear where a line ends. Lines darken as the game ages: faint openings, emphatic endings. Each work may be watched being made, move by move, at the tempo of the game itself.
